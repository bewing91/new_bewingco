A Streamlined way to follow companies

Research

Understanding B2B salespeople's needs

In order to understand the needs of the users, we conducted a series of 10 user interviews with B2B salespeople. Given that we were initially tasked with designing an iOS app, we were particularly interested in learning about their workflows and behaviors, and where (mobile vs desktop) they completed different activities.

Based on these conversations, we came away with some key insights:

  • Salespeople prefer to conduct research on their desktops
  • Salespeople monitor a list of companies and respond to updates about these companies
  • Notification needs and preferences vary. Salespeople rarely need to respond immediately.

After considering these insights and the Craft's goals and needs, we decided that improvements to Craft's new feed functionality would best create value for users and the business.
Identifying the problem

Users struggle to follow companies and identify information about companies they do follow.

To identify problems in the Craft feed, we conducted usability tests with 5 people. After uncovering a slew of issues in the current feed experience, we prioritized them using a 2x2 and plotted each problem based on ease of implementation and impact on the user.

We came away with 3 key problems to address:

  1. Finding companies to follow is confusing.
  2. Following companies is confusing.
  3. Identifying information in the feed is difficult.
1. Finding companies to follow is confusing.

How might we make it easy to find companies to follow?

Before
The original feed page had two search bars: one in the top navigation bar and one on the right side of the feed content.

The side search made it easy to follow companies from the page, but only 1/5 users we tested thought to search for a company with the side search rather than the top search.

This resulted in users being taken away from the feed and having to spend time locating the follow button on a company's profile.
Exploring solutions
With Craft's existing design system and patterns in mind, we explored different solutions to make finding companies to follow easier.

Among the ideas we considered were merging the two searches into one, and adding clearer headers to improve clarity.
After
I removed the side search to reduce confusion and in order to allow users to quickly and easily follow companies, added 'Follow' links to companies returned in the top nav search results.

The rest of the side search card was given a clear header 'Suggested Companies' that shared the styling of the other side card headers.
2. Following companies is confusing.

How might we make it easy to follow a company?

Before
The modal to follow companies was hard for users to understand. They struggled to understand how to add companies to following lists and weren't sure when a company was successfully followed. When they wanted to create a new list, they were taken to a separate screen, leading to more confusion and dropoff.
Exploring Solutions
We wireframed a few different modal configurations including different options for creating a new list without leaving the modal.
After
We streamlined the modal by reducing the number of call-to-actions and settings on it. The header copy was changed to be more conversational in order make it easier to understand, as well as to account for both adding (following) and removing (unfollowing) modal states.

After the changes, 5/5 users tested understood how to follow a company, and knew when they had successfully done so.
3. Identifying information in the feed is difficult.

How might we make it easy to understand information in the feed?

Before
The original cards had high cognitive load. With the key information like the company name and type of update split into multiple headers and subheaders, and the date repeated on the news card, it was hard for users to process what was in each update.
Exploring Solutions
After sketching out a variety of options, we settled on two different options to test further: a conversational style header and a header that relied on badges.

We tested them with 40 people on UsabilityHub to gauge which was easier to understand. Based on our testing, it became clear that the conversational header required less interpretation and processing while the button-like nature of the badges led to some confusion.
After
The previous headers were consolidated into one conversational style header that users could read at a quick glance. The date format was changed to a more casual format as well to make it more accessible.
Outcome

Headed to production



Craft's team was so excited about the changes that we made, that they moved them to the top of the roadmap and plan to implement them in early Q4 2019. Thanks to our use of Craft's existing design system, the changes are relatively easy to implement and thus have a potentially high ROI for the Craft platform.
